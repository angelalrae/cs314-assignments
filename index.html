Food cultivates justice

A post shared by For The Love Of Food (@angela.cooks) on Oct 18, 2017 at 6:36am PDT

At Gonzaga, I am a leader of our Real Food Challenge (RFC) club. This means I am involved with educating students on sustainable food systems and working with our dining services provider, Sodexo, to get more ecologically-sound, local, fair, and humane food at our campus. This summer, I designed a website for us to communicate about the work we're doing, which you can see here. I'm working on creating tools for us to display the data we have on the food in our dining halls.

In October 2017, a group of four of us from RFC went to the AASHE sustainability conference in San Antonio to present on the intersectionality of food justice and how we can empower students to support a environmentally sustainable campus. For me, food started as a means of communicating culture and celebration, but through RFC it has also become a means toward a more justice and fair world.

Technology communicates values

In my work with Gonzaga's Digital Humanities Initiative (DHI), I've seen first-hand how we can use technology to support access to information. I started working for the Initiative in January 2016, as the primary web designer putting together a site to communicate the process and importance of an original musical created by our Theatre and Religious Studies departments. The show was created to tell the stories of women's voices, so the goal of the website was to further amplify those voices beyond the Spokane community to a wide range of audiences. To the right, is a video of me talking about how the project impacted me, which is on the WSV site.

This semester with DHI, I am working as a research intern and consultant on a new cultural/historical mapping project with our Spanish department. I enjoy the continued opportunity to help others use technology to communicate and visualize what is important to them.

Service begets community

I started my college career by attending a pre-orientation week-long program centered on community service opportunities at and around Gonzaga. From this, I learned about Gonzaga's Mission: Possible, an alternative spring break trip that sends students to sites around the country for a week of service. My freshman and sophomore years I participated on these trips, going to San Francisco, CA to learn about enviromental justice and to Tacoma, WA to work with the Catholic Worker House.

This year, I am leading a group of 13 on the Mission: Possible trip to Neah Bay, WA where we will be working with the Makah Nation Native American tribe. We will live in community with the Makah people, helping at an elementary school and their cultural center/museum. I greatly value service work, and it is wonderful to go to a Jesuit university that prioritizes this. After graduation, I hope to participate in a year of service through either the Jesuit Volunteer Corps or City Year.
